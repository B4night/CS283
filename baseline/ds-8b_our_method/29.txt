The fall of Constantinople to the Ottomans in 1453 marked a pivotal moment in history, a tragedy that brought an end to the Byzantine Empire and reshaped the course of the region. The city, known as the "Queen of Cities," was a marvel of engineering and a symbol of resilience, yet it couldn't withstand the relentless assault of the Ottoman Empire.

On April 6, 1453, the sun dipped below the horizon, casting a golden glow over the ancient city. The Theodosian Walls, once a testament to Byzantine ingenuity, stood as the final defense against the Ottoman army, commanded by the 21-year-old Sultan Mehmed II. The Ottomans, known for their relentless tactics and advanced artillery, had surrounded Constantinople, cutting off supplies and forcing the Byzantine defenders to rely on their dwindling resources. Inside the city, the population was in a state of anxiety, with shops closed and streets eerily quiet, the usual bustle replaced by the ominous silence of impending danger.
