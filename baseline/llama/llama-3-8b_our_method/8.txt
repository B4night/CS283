He began by quashing a rebellion that had taken place in the southern city-states, using his military prowess and strategic thinking to quickly and decisively put down the uprising.

With the rebellion crushed, Alexander turned his attention to the northern city-states, which had long been a thorn in the side of Macedonian power. He staged a swift and brutal campaign against these city-states, demonstrating his military might and earning the respect and admiration of his troops.

As he solidified his control over the city-states, Alexander began to turn his attention to the wider world beyond Greece. He had long been fascinated by the stories of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, which stretched from the Indus River in the east to the Mediterranean in the west. He saw an opportunity to expand Macedonian power and prestige by conquering the Persian Empire, and he began to make plans for a campaign against the empire's ruler, Darius III.

Alexander's vision was not limited to conquest alone, however. He also saw himself as a unifier, bringing together the disparate city-states of Greece under a single banner and promoting a sense of Greek identity and culture. He believed that by conquering the Persian Empire, he could spread Greek culture and values throughout the ancient world, and create a new era of peace and prosperity.

As he prepared for his campaign against the Persian Empire, Alexander was filled with a sense of excitement and anticipation.
