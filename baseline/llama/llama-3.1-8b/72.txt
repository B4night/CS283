The British government had announced its intention to grant independence to India and Pakistan, two separate nations that would be carved out of the Indian subcontinent.

Mahatma Gandhi, the father of the Indian independence movement, stood on the steps of the Red Fort in Delhi, his eyes fixed on the crowd of thousands gathered before him. He was flanked by Jawaharlal Nehru, the future Prime Minister of India, and Muhammad Ali Jinnah, the leader of the Muslim League and the future Governor-General of Pakistan.

As the clock struck midnight, Gandhi raised his hands to silence the crowd. "At the stroke of the midnight hour, when the world sleeps, India awakens to life and freedom," he declared, his voice ringing out across the city. "The whole of India is one and indivisible, and we shall work for the welfare of all its people, without distinction of caste, creed, or community."

The crowd erupted in cheers and applause as Nehru took the microphone to deliver his own address. "Long live India!" he shouted, his voice hoarse with emotion. "Long live the Indian people!"

Meanwhile, in Lahore, Pakistan's capital city, Jinnah stood on the steps of the Lahore High Court, addressing a crowd of his own. "Pakistan is born today," he declared, his voice trembling with pride. "We have fought for this moment for decades, and now we have achieved it. We will build a nation that is just, equitable, and free for all its citizens."
